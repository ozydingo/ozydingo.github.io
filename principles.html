The developer's approach is "how do I write a query or add an API call to get that information", while the architect's approach is "how can we make that information an obvious and natural consequence of the code design?".

To be clear, you need to blend both framings; the architect's approach is more costly up front and that can be not worth it if the product roadmap is rapidly pivoting. If, however, we believe we will continue to build on these developments with some momentum, the architect's approach pays off in the medium to long term. And that's what excites me the most: the ability to make an impact on that level, to mold architecture to fit an evolving understanding of our use cases such that very few feature requests require complex dev-gymnastics but instead are well-supported by a thoughtful and robust architecture.

The largest cost of writing software is reading it.
